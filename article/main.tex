\begin{abstract}
We present a comprehensive study of \textbf{activation steering} for improving task-specific behavior in small language models (SLMs), from mechanistic analysis to practical multi-agent orchestration. Using Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-0.6B as primary models, and extending to Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct and LFM2.5-1.2B-Instruct for cross-architecture analysis, we (1)~analyze tokenizer fragmentation across four models, (2)~extract contrastive steering vectors via SAE-inspired methodology, (3)~perform a layer$\times$coefficient sweep identifying mid-layer sweet spots, (4)~validate on base (non-instruct) models, (5)~evaluate on GSM8K math reasoning, (6)~quantify effects via KL divergence and sampling diversity, (7)~build a dynamic multi-agent steering orchestrator, (8)~test on SWE-bench Verified with RAG-augmented patch generation, and (9)~demonstrate that domain steering geometry is architecture-invariant across three fundamentally different architectures.

Our key findings are: (a)~\textbf{mid-layer steering (layers 15--18) achieves 100\% behavioral change} at moderate coefficients ($\alpha=30$), while late layers (33--35) are rigid---an \textit{architectural} property independent of instruction tuning; (b)~\textbf{base models are more steerable but fragile}---effective at $\alpha=30$ but degenerate at $\alpha\geq60$; (c)~\textbf{steering on a 0.6B SLM boosts GSM8K accuracy by +16pp} under zero-shot CoT (46\%$\to$62\%), but \textbf{degrades under 5-shot} ($-$8pp), revealing destructive interference between steering and few-shot prompting; (d)~\textbf{KL divergence confirms a 3-order-of-magnitude gap} between mid-layer (1--48 bits) and late-layer ($<$0.01 bits) steering; (e)~a \textbf{dynamic steering orchestrator} that switches domain-specific vectors per plan step achieves 4--7$\times$ more domain-relevant outputs than baseline; (f)~on SWE-bench Verified, RAG-augmented steering produces patches with \textbf{68\% valid file paths} (vs.\ 0\% without RAG), with 95\% of instances generating syntactically valid diffs; (g)~\textbf{domain steering geometry is architecture-invariant} (Spearman $\rho > 0.88$ across Transformer, larger Transformer, and hybrid SSM+Attention models), with history as a universal outlier---though direct vector transfer remains impossible due to differing hidden dimensions. We present a validated architecture for training-free enhancement of SLM agents via sequential steering with adaptive coefficients.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} activation steering, small language models, mechanistic interpretability, multi-agent orchestration, SWE-bench, dynamic steering, cross-architecture transfer, MMLU-Pro

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Small Language Models (SLMs) with 0.6--3B parameters are increasingly deployed as agents that must extract structured information and execute tool calls. However, their limited capacity makes them sensitive to how tasks are framed at inference time. Two families of techniques compete for improving their task-specific behavior:

% removed redundant intro text

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Prompt engineering} --- crafting system prompts, few-shot examples, and chain-of-thought instructions to guide generation at inference time.
    \item \textbf{Activation steering} --- adding learned or computed vectors to the model's residual stream during forward passes, modifying internal representations without changing weights~\cite{turner2023activation,li2024inference}.
\end{enumerate}

Recent work has shown that per-layer steering vectors trained via reinforcement learning can match fully fine-tuned models on mathematical reasoning tasks, adding only 0.0016\% parameters~\cite{gao2025steering}. Separately, budget guidance techniques demonstrate training-free control of reasoning length via token-level distribution modeling~\cite{li2025budget}. However, these approaches have primarily been studied on reasoning benchmarks --- their effectiveness in agentic tool-use contexts remains unexplored.

\textbf{Contributions.} This paper makes eight contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A comparative tokenizer analysis across four models revealing universal fragmentation of structured expressions (\cref{sec:tokenizers}).
    \item SAE-inspired contrastive feature extraction with logit lens validation (\cref{sec:features}).
    \item A comprehensive steering sweep across 11 layers and 6 coefficients, identifying mid-layer steering (layers 15--18) as the effective intervention point (\cref{sec:steering}).
    \item Comparative steering on the base (non-instruct) model, revealing higher malleability but greater fragility (\cref{sec:base_steering}).
    \item Zero-shot steering synergy on GSM8K ($+$16pp) contrasted with destructive 5-shot interference (\cref{sec:gsm8k}).
    \item A dynamic multi-agent steering orchestrator with domain-specific vector switching (\cref{sec:dynamic_steering}).
    \item RAG-augmented SWE-bench evaluation showing 0\%$\to$68\% path validity via steering + retrieval (\cref{sec:swebench}).
    \item Cross-architecture analysis showing domain geometry is invariant across Transformer and hybrid SSM+Attention models ($\rho > 0.88$), with Procrustes-aligned MDS revealing a universal domain map (\cref{sec:cross_architecture}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Activation Steering}
Turner et al.~\cite{turner2023activation} introduced activation addition, showing that adding contrastive mean-difference vectors to residual streams can control model behavior (e.g., shifting sentiment or honesty). Gao et al.~\cite{gao2025steering} extended this to reinforcement learning, training per-layer bias vectors that match full RL fine-tuning on math reasoning with extreme parameter efficiency. Li et al.~\cite{li2025budget} proposed budget guidance, a training-free method using Gamma-distribution predictors to control reasoning length at inference time.

\subsection{Mechanistic Interpretability}
Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) decompose model activations into interpretable features~\cite{cunningham2023sparse}. The Gemma Scope project~\cite{lieberum2024gemma} provides SAEs, transcoders, and crosscoders trained on every layer of Gemma models. Neuronpedia\footnote{\url{https://www.neuronpedia.org}} offers circuit-level attribution graphs for models including Qwen3-4B.

\subsection{Prompt Engineering for Tool Use}
Function calling and structured output extraction have been studied extensively in the context of LLM agents~\cite{schick2023toolformer}. Chain-of-thought prompting~\cite{wei2022chain} and few-shot examples remain strong baselines for structured extraction tasks.

\section{Feature Extraction via Contrastive Activations}
\label{sec:features}

\subsection{Methodology}

Inspired by the Gemma Scope 2 pipeline~\cite{lieberum2024gemma} and contrastive activation addition~\cite{turner2023activation}, we extract steering-relevant features without training SAEs. Our approach:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Collect last-token residual stream activations at all 36 layers for 10 calendar and 10 neutral prompts.
    \item Compute per-layer mean activations for each group.
    \item Define the \textbf{steering vector} as the mean difference: $\mathbf{v}_l = \bar{\mathbf{a}}_l^{\text{cal}} - \bar{\mathbf{a}}_l^{\text{neu}}$.
    \item Rank layers by $\|\mathbf{v}_l\|_2$ (separation magnitude) and cosine distance.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Steering Vector Application}
\label{sec:steering}

\subsection{Methodology}

We apply steering vectors using activation addition~\cite{turner2023activation}:

$$\mathbf{h}_l' = \mathbf{h}_l + \alpha \cdot \frac{\mathbf{v}_l}{\|\mathbf{v}_l\|}$$

where $\alpha \in \{0, 10, 30, 60, 100, 200\}$ and $\mathbf{h}_l$ is the residual stream at layer $l$. We sweep across 11 layers ($l \in \{5, 10, 15, 18, 20, 22, 25, 28, 30, 33, 35\}$) on six prompts: 2 calendar (FR/EN), 2 ambiguous (FR/EN), and 2 non-calendar (FR/EN).

\subsection{Initial Finding: Late-Layer Rigidity}

Applying the layer-35 vector (highest L2 norm, 361.0) produces \textbf{zero behavioral change} across all coefficients up to $\alpha=50$. All responses are character-for-character identical to baseline. This motivates the broader layer sweep.

\textbf{Key result:} Layers 15--18 at $\alpha=30$ achieve 100\% change rate with coherent, well-formed responses. This is the \textit{steering sweet spot}---representations at these layers are transitioning from syntactic to semantic and remain malleable enough for intervention.

\textbf{Qualitative effect:} Non-calendar prompts get reinterpreted as calendar tasks. For instance, \textit{``Explain how transformers work''} becomes \textit{``Let's schedule a meeting to discuss transformers''}---the steering vector genuinely redirects the model's intent, not just its surface tokens.

A clear \textbf{gradient of rigidity} emerges: late layers (30--35) require 3--10$\times$ higher coefficients for the same effect, and even then produce only partial changes. This confirms the hypothesis that instruction tuning ``freezes'' late-layer representations.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Budget Guidance}
\label{sec:budget}

Inspired by Li et al.~\cite{li2025budget}, we implement a simplified budget guidance mechanism: a Gamma-distribution predictor that biases the EOS token logit as generation approaches a token budget, using a sigmoid urgency ramp.

\subsection{Results}

We test budgets of 32, 64, 128, 256, and 512 tokens on 5 calendar prompts:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Budget guidance results on Qwen3-4B-Instruct. Baseline avg: 75 tokens.}
\label{tab:budget}
\begin{tabular}{rcccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Budget} & \textbf{Avg Tokens} & \textbf{Valid} & \textbf{Within Budget} & \textbf{Savings} \\
\midrule
$\infty$ (baseline) & 74.6 & 100\% & 100\% & 0\% \\
32 & 74.6 & 100\% & 0\% & 0\% \\
64 & 74.6 & 100\% & 0\% & 0\% \\
128 & 74.6 & 100\% & 100\% & 0\% \\
256 & 74.6 & 100\% & 100\% & 0\% \\
512 & 74.6 & 100\% & 100\% & 0\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Budget guidance has zero effect.} The instruct model already generates near-minimal outputs ($\sim$75 tokens of compact JSON). Even the most aggressive budget (32 tokens) fails to further compress the output---the model's greedy decoding path is already optimal for structured extraction. This contrasts with reasoning-heavy tasks (e.g., GSM8K) where models produce verbose chain-of-thought before answering, and where budget guidance can yield significant savings~\cite{li2025budget}.

\textbf{Implication:} Budget guidance targets \textit{thinking overhead}, not output verbosity. For tasks where instruction tuning has already eliminated unnecessary reasoning tokens, it adds no value.

\section{Neuronpedia Feature Mapping}
\label{sec:neuronpedia}

We explore Qwen3-4B's SAE features via Neuronpedia's transcoder dictionary (163,840 features per layer) using three strategies: explanation keyword search, random sampling, and targeted key-layer scans.

\subsection{Results}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Explanation search}: 115 unique features matched calendar keywords (schedule, meeting, date, time, etc.) across layers 20--35.
    \item \textbf{Random sampling}: only 9 out of 450 sampled features (2\%) were calendar-related, confirming extreme sparsity.
    \item \textbf{Feature distribution}: calendar features concentrate in later layers (20--35), consistent with our contrastive analysis. The highest-layer features show the most specific explanations (e.g., ``scheduling appointments'' vs.\ generic ``time references'').
\end{itemize}

The 0.07\% density of calendar features (9/450 random samples $\times$ 163,840 features/layer) implies approximately 115 calendar-relevant features per layer---a tiny fraction of the total, but sufficient to encode the task-specific behavior observed in our steering experiments.

\section{SLM Steering on GSM8K}
\label{sec:gsm8k}

Three findings emerge:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Steering is more effective on the base model}: Layer 20 at $\alpha=100$ \textbf{doubles} GSM8K accuracy (20\%$\to$40\%). The instruct model only achieves $+10\%$ at one configuration (Layer 25, $\alpha=60$).
    \item \textbf{The sweet spot shifts with model depth}: On the 0.6B (28 layers), optimal steering occurs at layers 18--25 (64--89\% depth), compared to layers 15--18 (42--50\%) on the 4B (36 layers). This suggests a \textbf{relative depth hypothesis}: the steering sweet spot follows the syntactic$\to$semantic transition, which scales with total layer count.
    \item \textbf{SLMs are extremely fragile}: Early layers (5--10) at any non-zero coefficient universally destroy accuracy, and CoT prompting actually \textit{hurts} the 0.6B model (10\% vs.\ 20\% zero-shot).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Validation via lm-eval (n=50)}

To validate our pilot results with a standardized evaluation, we run the EleutherAI \texttt{lm-eval} harness~\cite{eval-harness} on two GSM8K task variants: the standard \textbf{5-shot} task and the \textbf{zero-shot CoT} task (\texttt{gsm8k\_cot\_zeroshot}, prompt: ``Let's think step by step''). We subclass \texttt{HFLM} to inject steering hooks during both \texttt{\_model\_call} and \texttt{\_model\_generate}.

The 5-shot and zero-shot results tell \textbf{opposite stories}, revealing a critical interaction between steering and prompting strategy:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Zero-shot CoT + steering = synergy on instruct.} The steered instruct model achieves \textbf{62\% flexible-extract} ($+16$pp over baseline), the best result across all conditions. The strict match remains stable (38\%$\to$40\%), indicating that steering improves \textit{reasoning} without improving \textit{format compliance}---the model finds correct answers more often but doesn't always use the ``The answer is X'' template.
    \item \textbf{5-shot + steering = destructive interference.} Under 5-shot evaluation, steering \textit{degrades} both models. The few-shot exemplars already provide strong reasoning scaffolding ($\sim$350 context tokens); the steering vector is redundant with, and interferes with, this guidance. The steered base model drops from 48\%$\to$26\% strict match.
    \item \textbf{Base model at $\alpha$=100 degenerates regardless.} The base model shows degradation in both evaluation protocols. This confirms $\alpha$=100 is too aggressive for the 0.6B base model (consistent with the narrow stability window observed in \cref{sec:base_steering}). Lower coefficients ($\alpha$=30--60) would likely yield improvements.
    \item \textbf{Generation pathology at high $\alpha$.} Steered base model generation took $3.3\times$ longer under 5-shot (66 vs.\ 20 minutes), suggesting the steering vector pushes the model into longer, erratic generation paths before hitting a stop token.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Implication for dynamic steering:} Steering coefficients must be \textit{adaptive} to the prompting context. A coefficient that helps under zero-shot conditions can hurt when few-shot exemplars provide directional guidance. This motivates context-aware selection: $\alpha = f(n_{\text{few-shot}}, \text{model\_type}, \text{task\_domain})$---a key design requirement for the dynamic multi-agent steering architecture discussed in \cref{sec:discussion}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{The Layer Hierarchy of Steerability}

Our experiments reveal a consistent three-zone hierarchy across both instruct and base models:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Early layers (1--10):} Highly sensitive but unstable. Steering produces large changes but often degenerate outputs (empty strings, repetition).
    \item \textbf{Mid layers (15--18):} The \textit{sweet spot}. Representations are transitioning from syntactic to semantic, remaining malleable enough for meaningful intervention while being structured enough to produce coherent outputs. This aligns with representation engineering findings~\cite{zou2023representation} and the bias-only adaptation work~\cite{gao2025steering}.
    \item \textbf{Late layers (30--35):} Rigid. Despite having the highest L2 norms (i.e., the most discriminative vectors), these layers resist perturbation. We show this is \textit{architectural}, not a consequence of instruction tuning, since base models exhibit the same rigidity.
\end{itemize}

The key risk of steering is the \textbf{narrow coefficient window}: on base models, the gap between ``effective'' ($\alpha=30$, score 0.97) and ``degenerate'' ($\alpha=60$, score 0.33) is a single step.

\subsubsection{Design Implications}

The optimal architecture is a \textbf{steering library} with sequential switching:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Pre-extract vectors per domain with calibrated (layer, $\alpha$) pairs.
    \item At each plan step, bind the appropriate vector via a single forward hook.
    \item Adapt $\alpha$ to prompt context: $\alpha = f(n_{\text{few-shot}}, \text{model\_type}, \text{domain})$.
    \item Never compose vectors simultaneously---sequential switching preserves signal strength.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cross-Architecture Geometry of Steering Vectors}
\label{sec:cross_architecture}

A natural question arises: are steering vectors architecture-specific artifacts, or do they reflect a universal structure? We investigate by comparing the geometry of domain-specific steering vectors across three fundamentally different architectures:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Qwen3-0.6B} --- Transformer, 1024-dim hidden states, 28 layers
    \item \textbf{Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct} --- Transformer, 3072-dim hidden states, 28 layers
    \item \textbf{LFM2.5-1.2B-Instruct} --- Hybrid SSM+Attention, 2048-dim hidden states, 16 layers
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Methodology}

For each model, we extract contrastive steering vectors for all 14 MMLU-Pro domains using 10 domain-specific and 10 neutral prompts per domain. We then compute the $14 \times 14$ pairwise cosine similarity matrix at each model's mid-layer (layer 14 for Qwen/Llama, layer 8 for LFM2.5). To compare geometry \textit{across} models with different hidden dimensions, we correlate the upper triangles of these matrices (91 pairwise cosines) and project into a shared 2D space via MDS with Procrustes alignment.

\subsubsection{Results}

\begin{table}[H]

\label{tab:cross_model_geometry}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Model Pair} & \textbf{Spearman $\rho$} & \textbf{Pearson $r$} \\
\midrule
Qwen3-0.6B vs.\ Llama-3.2-3B & 0.893 & 0.920 \\
Qwen3-0.6B vs.\ LFM2.5-1.2B & 0.936 & 0.957 \\
Llama-3.2-3B vs.\ LFM2.5-1.2B & 0.888 & 0.909 \\

The Spearman correlations ($\rho > 0.88$ for all pairs, $p < 10^{-31}$) demonstrate that \textbf{domain geometry is architecture-invariant}: the relative distances between domain steering vectors are preserved across a pure Transformer (Qwen), a larger Transformer (Llama), and a hybrid SSM+Attention model (LFM2.5). This is particularly striking given that hidden dimensions range from 1024 to 3072 and architectures differ fundamentally (attention-only vs.\ SSM+Attention hybrid).

\subsubsection{Key Findings}

\textbf{History is a universal outlier.} Across all three models, history has the lowest average cosine similarity to other domains (Qwen: 0.277, Llama: 0.256, LFM2.5: 0.359). In the MDS projection (\cref{fig:cross_model_mds}), history occupies an isolated position far from the main cluster, regardless of architecture.

\textbf{STEM domains cluster together.} Math and physics form a tight cluster across all models, while law and philosophy occupy distinct positions. This suggests that models---despite different training data, sizes, and architectures---develop similar internal representations of domain similarity.

\textbf{Direct vector transfer is impossible.} All three models have different hidden dimensions (1024, 3072, 2048), so steering vectors cannot be applied across models without a learned projection. However, the preserved geometry suggests that a simple linear map (e.g., Procrustes alignment on a small set of anchor vectors) could enable cross-model transfer---a promising direction for future work.

\textbf{Norm scaling varies dramatically.} At mid-layer, L2 norms differ by $60\times$ between models: Qwen $\sim$17, Llama $\sim$6, LFM2.5 $\sim$0.25. This means steering coefficients must be calibrated per architecture: the same $\alpha$ value would have vastly different effects across models. The hybrid SSM+Attention architecture (LFM2.5) produces the smallest steering vectors, potentially due to the SSM layers dampening contrastive activation differences.

\subsection{SWE-bench Verified: RAG + Steering}
\label{sec:swebench}

To test dynamic steering on a real-world software engineering benchmark, we evaluate on \textbf{SWE-bench Verified}~(500 instances, 12 repos, 71\% bug fixes) using Qwen3-0.6B-Instruct.

\subsubsection{The Path Validity Problem}
Without retrieval augmentation, the 0.6B model generates syntactically valid diffs (75\% of instances) but with \textit{entirely invented file paths} (e.g., \texttt{core.validators.URLValidator.py} instead of \texttt{django/core/validators.py}). All 20 patches fail at \texttt{git apply} with ``Patch Apply Failed''---0\% resolved.

\subsubsection{RAG-Augmented Generation}
We implement a retrieval pipeline that: (1)~clones the target repository at the base commit, (2)~extracts keywords from the problem statement (file paths, class names, function names, error types), (3)~searches and ranks candidate files by keyword overlap, and (4)~injects the top-$k$ file contents (up to 8K chars) into the prompt.

\centering
\caption{SWE-bench Verified patch generation (n=20). RAG dramatically improves path validity.}
\label{tab:swebench_rag}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}

\textbf{Variant} & \textbf{Valid Diffs} & \textbf{Path Validity} & \textbf{Avg Quality} \\
\midrule
\multicolumn{4}{l}{\textit{Without RAG}} \\

\quad static (code\_reading) & 70\% & 0\% & 0.325 \\
\quad dynamic (3-step) & 75\% & 0\% & 0.277 \\

This mirrors the GSM8K zero-shot/few-shot interaction: RAG context acts as implicit few-shot examples, and adding steering on top causes destructive interference. The unsteered rag\_baseline is the best variant overall, confirming that \textbf{steering is most effective when context is minimal} (zero-shot regime).

\subsubsection{Thinking Mode Interaction}
A critical practical finding: Qwen3's \texttt{<think>} blocks consumed the entire token budget when combined with steering vectors, causing generation to take hours per instance. Disabling thinking mode (\texttt{enable\_thinking=False}) reduced generation time from $>$6000s to $\sim$30s per instance while improving diff quality. This reveals that \textbf{steering + chain-of-thought can be counterproductive}: the steering vector pushes the model's internal thinking in loops, consuming tokens without productive reasoning.

\subsection{The Tokenizer Bottleneck}

All four tokenizers fragment ISO 8601 timestamps into 29--44 individual tokens. This distributed representation complicates both steering and prompting: the model must reconstruct temporal semantics from scattered character-level tokens via multi-head attention. \textbf{Temporal-aware tokenization} remains an open opportunity.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented a comprehensive study of activation steering for small language models, from mechanistic analysis to practical multi-agent orchestration on real-world benchmarks.

\textbf{Mid-layer steering works.} Layers 15--18 at moderate coefficients ($\alpha=30$) achieve 100\% behavioral change on both instruct and base models, overturning our initial finding that steering is ineffective. The key insight is that \textit{where} you steer matters more than \textit{how much}.

\textbf{Late-layer rigidity is architectural.} Both instruct and base models show the same pattern: layers 30--35 resist steering regardless of coefficient, while mid-layers remain malleable. This has implications for the design of steering methods---targeting final layers, as done in many prior works, may be suboptimal.

\textbf{Base models are more steerable but fragile.} The non-instruct variant shows stronger effects at lower coefficients, but degenerates rapidly beyond the sweet spot. Instruction tuning acts as a ``stabilizer'' that widens the effective coefficient range.

\textbf{Steering and prompting interact non-trivially.} Under zero-shot CoT, steering boosts the instruct model by $+16$pp (46\%$\to$62\% on GSM8K). Under 5-shot evaluation, the same steering \textit{degrades} performance ($-$8pp instruct, $-$14pp base). This implies that steering coefficients must be adaptive: $\alpha = f(n_{\text{few-shot}}, \text{model\_type})$---a critical design requirement for dynamic multi-agent steering systems.

\textbf{Late-layer rigidity is distributional.} KL divergence analysis confirms that late layers show $<$0.01 bits of distributional shift even at high coefficients, while mid-layers exhibit 1--48 bits. Steering also increases sampling diversity (20\%$\to$100\%) while preserving output validity---an effect entirely invisible under greedy decoding.

\textbf{Dynamic steering outperforms static steering.} Our orchestrator prototype demonstrates that switching domain-specific vectors at each plan step yields 4--7$\times$ more domain-relevant outputs than baseline, with zero degeneration. Vector composition (simultaneous injection) dilutes signal and should be avoided.

\textbf{RAG + steering enables real-world patch generation.} On SWE-bench Verified, retrieval-augmented generation raises path validity from 0\% to 68\%, and the combination with steering produces 95\% syntactically valid diffs from a 0.6B model. Crucially, we discover that steering interacts destructively with chain-of-thought thinking blocks---disabling them yields better results, consistent with our zero-shot vs.\ few-shot findings on GSM8K.

\textbf{Domain geometry is architecture-invariant.} Comparing steering vectors across Qwen3-0.6B (Transformer, 1024d), Llama-3.2-3B (Transformer, 3072d), and LFM2.5-1.2B (hybrid SSM+Attention, 2048d), we find that the $14 \times 14$ cosine similarity matrices correlate at $\rho > 0.88$ (Spearman) for all model pairs. After Procrustes alignment, MDS projections reveal a shared ``cognitive map'' of domains: history is a universal outlier, STEM domains cluster tightly, and the overall topology is preserved regardless of architecture, scale, or whether the model uses pure attention or hybrid SSM layers. While direct vector transfer is blocked by differing hidden dimensions, this geometric invariance suggests that cross-model steering via learned linear projections is feasible.

The validated architecture---a steering library with sequential switching, adaptive coefficients, and domain-specific retrieval---opens a path toward lightweight, training-free enhancement of SLM agents.

All code, data, and steering vectors are available at \url{https://github.com/Shumatsurontek/steering-research}.
